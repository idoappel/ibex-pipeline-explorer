What am I looking at? (click to hide)

A processor runs your program one instruction at a time — tiny commands like "add these two numbers" or "fetch that value from memory". To go faster, the chip works like an assembly line with 3 stations: while one instruction is being executed, the next one is already being fetched from memory, and the previous one is saving its result. So up to 3 instructions are inside the chip at once. Each tick of this assembly line is one clock cycle.

Registers are 32 tiny storage slots inside the CPU (named x0–x31). Almost every instruction reads one or two of them and writes one back.

How to use this page: click any instruction on the left, then use ▶ or the step buttons to walk it through the three stations. The diagram shows the actual electrical signal values recorded from the simulation at every cycle.
